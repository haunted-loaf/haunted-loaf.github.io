Corporate Congress

After the republic started to seize franchise assets, the directors of a number of franchise-and-concession-holding corporations convened the corporate congress on Arcadia. In possession of large parts of the union's navy and economic backbone, these corporations are in a position to declare and defend their independence.

The congress operates a shareholder system, where each member has voting power according to how many congressional shares it holds. Most individuals are not shareholders - a single share costs a year's wages.

The member corporations are generally free to run their internal affairs as they wish: the congress is chiefly concerned with mediating disputes, enabling cooperation between members, and, most importantly, supporting mutual defence against enemies of corporate rule.

As the corporate charters the corporations were founded under were null and void, residents under corporate jurisdiction are treated as having no citizenship. They have little or no political power, with the political and corporate structure of their society being one and the same. Most corporate residents are paid in company scrip, and cannot easily engage in economic activity outside their conglomerate.

Residents in private incorporated settlements may enjoy a better quality of life and some political power, but this is limited by the conditions of entry. A new member must buy their way in by obtaining congressional shares from a current holder, who will expect to be handsomely compensated for it.

Those residents who are paid in congressional credits may eventually be able to afford to buy a congressional share. For the average worker, a congressional share may cost as much as ten year's wages. The share comes with a sliver of polical power, as well as corporate citizenship. Corporations are always keen to purchase congressional shares in private hands.
